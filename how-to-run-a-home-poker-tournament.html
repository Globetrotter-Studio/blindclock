How to run a home poker tournament

Updated July 2026 · A practical guide for 4–20 players

A smooth home poker tournament comes down to five decisions made before the first hand: the buy-in, the payout split, how many chips each player gets, the blind structure, and who — or what — runs the clock. This guide gives you working numbers for each one.

What you need

  • A chip set — plan on about 50 chips per player in three or four denominations. A standard 300-chip case comfortably covers 6–8 players.
  • Two decks of cards and a dealer button — one deck shuffles while the other is in play.
  • A table — up to 9 or 10 players per table; beyond that, start with two tables and combine later.
  • A tournament clock everyone can see — an iPad or iPhone running BlindClock propped where the whole table can read it. Blind levels, breaks, and sounds run themselves.
  • The rules, written down — buy-in, rebuy window, and payout split, agreed before hand one.

Set the buy-in and the payout structure

For 10 players or fewer, pay the top three finishers 50/30/20 percent of the prize pool; for 11–20 players, pay four places — roughly 50/25/15/10. A good rule of thumb is that about one in five entrants should get paid: fewer feels top-heavy, more makes winning feel pointless.

Most home games run a fixed buy-in in the $20–$50 range. Decide the rebuy policy up front: a typical rule is rebuys allowed only during the first three or four levels, at the same price as the buy-in. How many chips does each player need?

About 50 chips per player, with the starting big blind at roughly 1% of the starting stack. The classic setup is a 5,000 stack with 25/50 blinds — a comfortable 100 big blinds, the same depth most casino events use. A clean per-player breakdown: 8 × 25, 8 × 100, 8 × 500 gives exactly 5,000 in 24 chips, leaving the rest of the case for rebuys and color-ups. For a deep-stack night, double the stack to 10,000 and keep the 25/50 start — 200 big blinds.

Three or four denominations are plenty. Once the smallest chip is no longer needed for the blinds, color it up at the next break: exchange small chips for larger ones so the stacks stay countable.

Pick a blind structure

Level length sets the pace of the whole night: 15–20 minutes per level for a fast turbo, 20–30 minutes for a standard home tournament, 30–45 minutes or more for a deep-stack event. Blinds should rise 25–50% per level and stick to round numbers. Here is what that looks like in practice:

Run the clock on the night

  • Draw for seats, highest card takes the dealer button.
  • Close the rebuy window on schedule — announce the last rebuy level as it starts.
  • Break every four or five levels for about 10 minutes; color up the smallest chips during the break.
  • Let the clock make the announcements — level-change sounds and a visible next-blinds line stop the “what are the blinds?” questions. On an iPad in landscape, BlindClock shows a full-table display and keeps the screen awake.
  • Watch the average stack in big blinds — when it drops below about 20, the endgame has started; below 10 and it’s a shove-fest, so consider slightly longer levels next time.

The endgame: deals and paying out

Combine tables once the total drops to a single table’s worth of players. When the blinds get huge relative to the stacks, the remaining players may prefer a deal instead of playing it out: the simple fair method is to pay everyone the next prize down, then split the remainder in proportion to chip counts. Any deal needs every remaining player’s agreement — otherwise play on. Pay the prizes from the pool immediately, in front of everyone.

Common mistakes to avoid

  • Deciding payouts after the bubble — agree on the split before hand one, not when the money is close.
  • Too few large chips — without 500s and 1000s you can’t color up, and late-night stacks become uncountable towers.
  • Vague rebuy rules — a fixed window and price prevents the classic mid-game argument.
  • Levels that don’t match the goal — 15-minute levels end a “relaxed evening” by 9pm; 40-minute levels keep everyone hostage past 2am.
  • Nobody owning the clock — a kitchen timer gets forgotten between hands. Use a dedicated tournament clock with automatic level changes and sounds.
